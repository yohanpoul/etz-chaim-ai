### Introduction

The Almadel (or Almandal , al-Mandal , Almādel , almadael , almodal , Almodellum Salomonis ,
Sefer ha-Almadil , מנדל)
referred to in this text is a kind of portable altar used
for consecrations and for summoning spirits. 1
The altar is elevated above an incense burner, and holes ( foramina ) allow the smoke to pass
through. According to the Latin text, the spirits will appear floating in the air above the almadel
(two cannas = approx. 16 palms).

(See demo prepared by Drs. Frank Klaassen and David Porreca ).
Versions of the text go back at least to the thirteenth century, and vary widely. 2
Pingree theorized the text was translated into Latin in the twelfth century
from a now-lost Arabic version, but the evidence is not strong, and debate continues.
It is possible the earliest germs were Sanskrit or even Persian. 3

In addition to the unique version published by Vajra Regan, two main versions are known,
distinguished as Almandal and Almadel . The former has more Arabic elements,
and the Almandal is made of brass ( aeris rubrum ) or on the ground,
while the latter has many more Christian elements and the Almadel is made of wax.

The English versions (presented below) descended from the Christian Almadel branch.
The English text has been simplified from its Latin ancestor, and incorporates the use of a crystal ball;
this contradicts the text's assertion that the spirits will appear floating in the air.
The English versions are also reduced to only four altitudes. The first 2 correspond with those
in the Latin Almadel, and the third corresponds roughly with the eighth. The last altitude in the English
almadel doesn't seem to correspond to any of those in the Latin almadel,
and the names are uncharacteristic of the others,
suggesting it might be a later interpolation to compensate for a defective text.

### Ritual apparatus 1: The Almadel figure

The almadel should be made from clean wax, one-half foot square. It should be colored as
appropriate for the altitude being worked with (see table 1 ).
Sacred names should be written on it with a new silver stylus.

Almost all of these names can be found in the Vinculum Salomonis , an early
Solomonic magic text which figures largely in
the Key of Solomon ,
Lucidarium artis Nigromanticae § XVII,
Heptameron , and other magic texts.
These include:
El, Yava (seems to appear in corrupted forms Yara and Yatha)
Adonay, Eloy, Heloym, Saday, Agla, Yoth, Elyon, Primeumathon, Ya, Onoytheon, and Anabona.

Honoytheon appears in various forms as (SSM, L.2.f.55): Onoytheon,
as well as Onaypheon, Onayefeon, and Oneyteon.

(Chave-Mahir and Véronèse 2015 p. 162 and p. 162 n 152.)

"Yoth Beth" is probably a corruption of "Yoth Heth", as it appears in the Halle ms.
This leaves only "Pius" unaccounted for.

All of the early manuscripts vary somewhat in the details of the names and other design elements:

Ms. W (based on description):
(In the first quadrant): Adonay, Alyas, Thelyon, Eloy, Pius.
(Second): Helyon, Heloim, Helym.
(Third): Yoth, Beth, agla
(Fourth): Thetagramathon, Saday, Ya .

Note however, according to the description in the text, there should be only 12 divine names around the perimeter —
one for each of the twelve altitudes (§§ 21-22; Véronèse 2012, pp. 145-146), thus showing that the
list of names assigned to the East is defective here.

In the corners should be the "sign of Solomon."
According to Véronèse, these are six- angled stars, but the manuscript doesn't use the

word sexangulus/exangulus here, but it does for the central figure,
and other manuscripts variously show five or six-pointed stars.
Between those signs should be written: Honoyteon, Pneumathon, Yatha, and Anabona .

Ms. W2, based on description:

In the second: Helyon, Heloym, Helym .
In the third quadrant: Yoth, Beth, Agla .

And it should be known that between one square and the next must be written the name [*sign] of Solomon ...
between the signs of the first quadrant this name should be written: Honoytheon ,
and between the signs of the second quadrant: Pneumathon ,
and between the signs of the third quadrant: Yatha ,
and between the signs of the fourth quadrant: Anabona .

### Ritual apparatus 2: The candles.

According to the Latin text,
"four candle holders should be made from the same wax,
such that two parts should be above the almadel, and the third part below.
Likewise there should be a lip on each candle holder on which the holy almadel can rest,
and four candles should be made from the same wax, and in the top of each candle holder
should be a hole, in which the candle should be placed." (Véronèse § 7 p. 137, my translation.)

The English manuscripts seem to have confused this, and instead state that one third of the wax should be used for
the almadel, and two thirds for the candles.

### Ritual apparatus 3: The garments.

According to the Latin text, the artiste should wear a garment that is the appropriate color
for the altitude, namely, the same color as the almadel and candles. (Véronèse § 20 pp. 144-145)

### Ritual apparatus 4: Incense and incense burner

The English version mentions only mastic as the incense of choice.

The Latin specifies mastic in most cases, but for the rest:

- fourth: saffron and frankincense

- sixth: mastic and frankincense

- seventh: "incense" ( thimiamata = Exod. 30:34?; / Coxe 25: cinnamon) and frankincense ( thus )

- eighth: frankincense, mastic, and musk

- nineth: musk, saffron, and frankincense

- tenth: lignum aloes and white pepper

### Ritual apparatus 5: The signet or golden seal.

 

According to the Latin text, a signet or seal should be made of gold or silver,
and placed in the middle of the almadel.
On it should be engraved sacred names, inverted .
( verso modo or everso modo . See Véronèse § 13, pp. 140-141,
and § L, p. 167.)
The reason for this is explained later in the ritual: If the angel fails to appear,
the conjurer should press the seal onto one of the candles, thus imprinting the sacred names
in their proper form into the wax.

Mss. W and W2 give the sacred names as " Helyon, Adonay ".
The Halle ms. reads " Heloen Adonay " in text, but Heloem Adonay in drawing;
the Vatican ms. " Eloem Adonay ";
Coxe 25 reads "these three names: Heloym, Hel, Adonay ."
BSB Cgm 407 similarly reads "dise drey nomen Heloym, Hel Adoney ."

It isnt clear whether Heloym (Elohim) אלהים, God/god
or Helyon עליון Most High was originally intended.

Perhaps the extra name here — El/Hel — (also seen in the English mss.)
was displaced from the hexagram in the earlier mss., and not originally part of the gold seal.

As seen above, ms. H (fol. 243r) depicts the golden seal as "perfectly circular" (" totius rotundum "),
both in the drawing and the text,
whereas the English manuscripts show it as triangular.

(reversed)

(or in Hebrew characters)

The early 16th century Hebrew ms. Genève 145 shows it in the shape of a pentagram,
with the names "BR (?) / ShU (?) / AVN (?) / ADNI / ALHIM."

NOTE: As with the other examples, the holes in the four corners are for the candles.
There are six incense vents at the points of the hexagram. The star-shaped signet shown in the
center is for stamping the candles, and is not part of the almadel table itself.

According to ms. H2 (fol. 230r) the sigil ring should be wrapped in red muslim ( sindon rubea ) when not in use.
